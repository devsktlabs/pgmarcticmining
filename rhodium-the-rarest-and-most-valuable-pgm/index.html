Rhodium: The Rarest and Most Valuable PGM

By Dr. Pavel Novak · 2026-03-23

rhodiumpgmskaergaardrare metals

Of the six platinum group metals, rhodium is the rarest and has historically been among the most expensive. Its extreme scarcity, concentrated supply, and irreplaceable industrial applications make it one of the most valuable metals in the world. Skaergaard's rhodium content, while not the primary driver of project economics, adds a high-value byproduct that enhances overall returns.

Rhodium Basics

  • **Symbol**: Rh
  • **Atomic number**: 45
  • **Density**: 12.41 g/cm3 (similar to silver)
  • **Melting point**: 1,964C (higher than palladium and platinum)
  • **Annual production**: Approximately 24-28 tonnes globally (770,000-900,000 ounces)
  • **Price range**: Historically US$5,000-30,000 per ounce (highly volatile); approximately US$5,000-7,000 in 2026

    • Where Rhodium Comes From

    Rhodium supply is extremely concentrated:

  • **South Africa**: Approximately 80-85% of global rhodium production, almost entirely as a byproduct of platinum-palladium mining in the Bushveld Complex
  • **Russia**: Approximately 10-12% of global supply from Norilsk Nickel
  • **Other**: Small amounts from recycling and minor producers

    • Rhodium is rarely the primary target of mining. It is produced as a byproduct of palladium and platinum mining, meaning rhodium supply is driven by PGM mining economics rather than rhodium-specific demand.

    Why Rhodium Is So Valuable

    Rhodium's high price is driven by supply-demand fundamentals:

  • **Extreme rarity**: Annual global production of only ~25 tonnes makes rhodium one of the rarest elements on Earth
  • **Irreplaceable**: Rhodium is the most effective catalyst for NOx reduction in automotive catalytic converters. No substitute matches its performance for this application
  • **Concentrated supply**: With 80%+ of supply from South Africa, any disruption to SA production causes severe supply shortages
  • **No primary mining**: Since rhodium is only produced as a byproduct, supply cannot easily be increased in response to price spikes

    • Rhodium Applications

    Automotive catalytic converters: Rhodium's primary use, accounting for approximately 80-85% of demand. Rhodium is the key catalyst for reducing nitrogen oxides (NOx) in exhaust gases. It is used in all types of catalytic converters (gasoline, diesel, and hybrid vehicles).

    Chemical industry: Rhodium catalysts are used in the production of:

  • Nitric acid (via the Ostwald process)
  • Acetic acid (Monsanto process)
  • Hydroformylation reactions for chemical synthesis

    • Glass manufacturing: Rhodium-platinum alloys are used in the production of fiberglass and glass fiber for wind turbine blades and insulation materials. The high-temperature resistance of rhodium alloys makes them ideal for glass-melting equipment.

    Electrical: Rhodium's excellent electrical conductivity and corrosion resistance make it useful in electrical contacts, spark plugs, and high-temperature wiring.

    Jewelry: Rhodium plating is widely used to give white gold and silver jewelry a bright, reflective finish. While the rhodium used in plating is very thin, the volume adds to total demand.

    Rhodium Price History

    Rhodium prices are among the most volatile of any commodity:

  • **2008**: Spiked above US$10,000/oz during the financial crisis, then crashed to US$1,000/oz
  • **2020-2021**: Spiked to over US$29,000/oz due to supply disruptions from COVID-19 and SA lockdowns
  • **2022-2023**: Declined sharply as automotive demand softened and SA supply recovered
  • **2024-2026**: Stabilized in the US$5,000-7,000 range as supply and demand came into better balance

    • The volatility reflects the thin market: with only ~25 tonnes of annual production, even small changes in supply or demand cause large price movements.

    Skaergaard's Rhodium Content

    Skaergaard contains rhodium as part of its PGM suite:

  • **Rhodium occurrence**: Rhodium is associated with the sulfide-rich mineralized zones alongside palladium and gold
  • **Grade**: Rhodium grades are lower than palladium but contribute meaningfully to metal value due to the high per-ounce price
  • **Recovery**: Rhodium is recovered through the same flotation processing as palladium and gold, with subsequent separation at the refinery
  • **Economic impact**: At US$5,000-7,000 per ounce, even modest rhodium grades generate significant per-tonne revenue, adding to the byproduct credit

    • The rhodium byproduct credit, combined with the gold credit, significantly improves Skaergaard's effective palladium production cost.

    Investment Implications

    Rhodium's presence in Skaergaard adds a layer of optionality and value:

  • **Price upside**: If rhodium prices spike (as they have historically), Skaergaard's rhodium revenue increases disproportionately, creating a windfall effect
  • **Diversification**: Three-metal exposure (palladium, gold, rhodium) provides diversification that single-metal projects cannot match
  • **Byproduct credit**: Rhodium revenue further reduces effective palladium AISC

    • The downside risk is that rhodium prices are volatile and could decline, but even at lower rhodium prices, the contribution to project economics remains positive.

    Conclusion

    Rhodium is the rarest and most strategically valuable PGM, with irreplaceable applications in automotive emissions control and industrial catalysis. Skaergaard's rhodium content adds a high-value byproduct that enhances project economics beyond palladium and gold alone. For investors, Skaergaard's three-metal PGM+Au suite provides diversified exposure to some of the world's most valuable precious metals, with rhodium adding optionality and potential upside that pure palladium projects cannot match.
