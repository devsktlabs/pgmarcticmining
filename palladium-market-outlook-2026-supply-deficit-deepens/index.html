Palladium Market Outlook 2026: Supply Deficit Deepens

By Dr. Pavel Novak · 2026-03-23

palladiummarket outlooksupply deficitpgm

The palladium market in 2026 is defined by a structural supply deficit that shows no signs of resolving. South African production continues its long-term decline, Russian supply faces sanctions-driven constraints, and demand from the automotive sector, while shifting toward platinum, remains substantial. For projects like Skaergaard that bring new Western supply, this deficit creates a favorable long-term market environment.

The Supply Side: Declining and Constrained

South Africa (70-75% of global supply): South Africa's palladium production has been in structural decline for over a decade. Key drivers include:

  • **Deepening mines**: SA's Bushveld Complex mines are getting deeper, hotter, and more expensive to operate. Declining grades at depth reduce output per tonne mined
  • **Energy crisis**: Load-shedding and unreliable grid power constrain mining operations and increase costs
  • **Labor challenges**: Wage negotiations, safety stoppages, and labor unrest periodically disrupt operations
  • **Capital discipline**: Major producers (Sibanye-Stillwater, Implats, Amplats) have shifted capital allocation toward safer, longer-life operations, reducing near-term production growth

    • Total South African PGM production has declined from a peak of approximately 6.5 million ounces palladium-equivalent per year to approximately 5.0-5.5 million ounces, and the trend continues downward.

    Russia (10-12% of global supply): Russian palladium supply from Norilsk Nickel faces ongoing challenges:

  • **Sanctions**: Western sanctions on Russia constrain Norilsk's access to technology, equipment, and financing, affecting production efficiency and growth plans
  • **Market access**: Russian palladium must find buyers through non-Western channels, creating supply chain complexity
  • **Operational challenges**: Norilsk faces its own geological and operational challenges, including declining grades at existing operations

    • Other supply: Secondary supply (recycling) from catalytic converter scrap provides approximately 2-2.5 million ounces annually, but growth is limited by the pace of vehicle fleet turnover.

    The Demand Side: Transitioning but Substantial

    Automotive catalytic converters: Palladium's primary demand driver is automotive catalytic converters for gasoline engines. Demand dynamics are shifting:

  • **Electrification**: The growth of electric vehicles (EVs) reduces long-term palladium demand for new vehicle production
  • **Gasoline hybrid growth**: Plug-in hybrids (PHEVs) and conventional hybrids use catalytic converters, sustaining palladium demand in the medium term
  • **Platinum substitution**: Some automakers are substituting platinum for palladium in catalytic converters due to the palladium price premium over platinum
  • **Emerging market vehicle growth**: Growing vehicle ownership in India, China, and Southeast Asia provides new palladium demand that partially offsets EV-related decline

    • Industrial demand: Palladium has important industrial applications beyond automotive:

  • Electronics: Used in multi-layer ceramic capacitors (MLCCs) and connector plating
  • Chemical industry: Palladium catalysts used in chemical manufacturing
  • Dentistry: Palladium alloys used in dental applications
  • Jewelry: Growing use of palladium in jewelry, particularly in China

    • Investment demand: Palladium investment (bars, coins, ETFs) provides additional demand, though it is smaller and more volatile than gold investment demand.

    The Deficit Math

    Putting supply and demand together:

  • **Total supply**: Approximately 7.5-8.0 million ounces (primary mine supply + recycling)
  • **Total demand**: Approximately 8.0-8.5 million ounces (automotive + industrial + investment)
  • **Deficit**: Approximately 0.5-1.0 million ounces annually

    • The deficit is being filled by destocking (drawing down above-ground palladium inventories). However, above-ground stocks are finite, and continued deficits will eventually require either demand destruction (higher prices) or new supply development.

    Price Implications

    The structural deficit supports palladium prices:

  • **Price floor**: The cost of marginal supply (high-cost South African mines) sets a price floor around US$800-900/oz. Below this level, South African producers would cut production, reducing supply and supporting prices
  • **Price ceiling**: The level at which demand destruction occurs (substitution to platinum, reduced autocatalyst loading) sets a ceiling around US$1,200-1,500/oz
  • **Current price**: Palladium trading around US$900-1,000/oz in 2026 is within this range, suggesting a balanced-to-slight-deficit market

    • Skaergaard's Position in the Market

    Skaergaard's palladium production potential positions it favorably in the supply-constrained market:

  • **Western supply**: As new Western-aligned supply, Skaergaard's palladium would flow through non-Russian, non-South-African channels, providing supply diversification
  • **Scale**: With 25.4 million PdEq ounces, Skaergaard is large enough to make a meaningful supply contribution
  • **Timing**: If production begins in the early 2030s, it enters the market during a period of likely deepening deficit as SA production continues to decline
  • **Gold hedge**: The gold byproduct credit provides a hedge against palladium-specific demand risks (substitution, electrification)

    • Risks to the Bull Case

    Key risks to the palladium supply deficit thesis:

  • **Faster-than-expected EV adoption**: If EV market share grows faster than projected, palladium demand could decline more quickly
  • **Platinum substitution acceleration**: If more automakers switch from palladium to platinum, demand could shift to platinum
  • **South African production recovery**: If SA producers resolve operational challenges and increase production, the deficit could narrow
  • **Russian supply normalization**: If sanctions are lifted or Norilsk finds alternative market access, supply could increase

    • Conclusion

    The palladium market faces a structural supply deficit driven by declining South African production, constrained Russian supply, and the gradual shift in automotive demand from palladium to platinum and EVs. This deficit supports palladium prices at levels that make new supply development economic. Skaergaard, with its 25.4 million PdEq ounces and Western jurisdiction, is well-positioned to capture value from this supply-constrained market. For investors, the palladium deficit thesis supports the long-term investment case for Skaergaard.
