PGM Geology 101: Understanding Layered Mafic Intrusions

By Dr. Pavel Novak · 2026-03-23

Most of the world's platinum group metals come from a single geological setting: layered mafic intrusions. Understanding what these formations are, how they form, and why they host PGM mineralization is fundamental to evaluating PGM investments like Skaergaard.

What Is a Layered Mafic Intrusion?

Under the leadership of Founder and CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines is advancing Skaergaard through the permitting and development pathway.

A layered mafic intrusion is a large body of igneous rock formed when molten magma (mafic composition, meaning rich in iron and magnesium, relatively low in silica) slowly cools and crystallizes within the Earth's crust. As the magma cools, minerals crystallize in a specific sequence, settling to the bottom of the magma chamber or floating to the top based on their density. This process creates distinct horizontal layers, hence the name "layered intrusion."

The key characteristics of layered mafic intrusions:

  • **Large size**: Typically many kilometers in length, width, and thickness. The Bushveld Complex is approximately 400 x 300 x 7 kilometers
  • **Layered structure**: Distinct horizontal layers of different rock types, each representing a different stage of crystallization
  • **Mafic composition**: Rich in iron, magnesium, calcium, and relatively poor in silica compared to granitic rocks
  • **Slow cooling**: The large size and insulating effect of surrounding rock means cooling takes millions of years, allowing large crystals to form and minerals to separate by density

    • How PGMs End Up in Layered Intrusions

    PGMs are concentrated in layered intrusions through a process called "sulfide saturation and collection":

  • **Magma generation**: Deep in the Earth's mantle, partial melting generates mafic magma that contains small amounts of dissolved PGMs and base metals (iron, nickel, copper, sulfur)
  • **Magma ascent and emplacement**: The magma rises through the crust and is emplaced as a large chamber. Initially, the magma is undersaturated in sulfur, meaning all sulfur is dissolved in the magma
  • **Sulfide saturation**: As the magma cools and evolves (through crystallization of early minerals), it eventually reaches sulfur saturation. At this point, molten sulfide liquid (an immiscible liquid composed of iron, nickel, copper, and sulfur) begins to separate from the silicate magma
  • **PGM collection**: PGMs have a strong chemical affinity for sulfide liquid. As sulfide droplets form and settle through the magma, they scavenge PGMs from the surrounding silicate melt. This is the key enrichment process: the PGMs are chemically collected by the sulfide liquid
  • **Layer formation**: The sulfide-rich layers (now enriched in PGMs) settle to the floor of the magma chamber, forming the PGM-bearing reef horizons

    • This process explains why PGMs are concentrated in thin, laterally extensive layers within otherwise barren rock.

    Types of PGM Mineralization in Layered Intrusions

    Reef-type mineralization: The most economically important type. Thin (1-3 meter) layers of PGM-rich sulfide mineralization that extend laterally for kilometers. The Bushveld's Merensky Reef and UG2 Reef are examples.

    Disseminated mineralization: PGM-bearing sulfides disseminated over broader zones (10-100+ meters). Lower grade but larger tonnage. The Platreef (Bushveld northern limb) and parts of Skaergaard fall into this category.

    Pipe-type mineralization: Vertical pipe-like bodies of PGM-rich rock, formed by late-stage magma fluids. Examples include the Bushveld's Driekop and Onverwacht pipes.

    The World's Major Layered Intrusions

    Bushveld Complex (South Africa): The world's largest and most economically important layered intrusion. Source of approximately 70-75% of global PGM supply. Hosts the Merensky Reef, UG2 Reef, and Platreef.

    Great Dyke (Zimbabwe): The second-largest PGM-hosting intrusion. Approximately 550 kilometers long and 4-11 kilometers wide. Hosts the Main Sulfide Zone (MSZ) with PGM mineralization similar to Bushveld.

    Stillwater Complex (Montana, USA): Approximately 48 kilometers long. Hosts the J-M Reef, a 1-3 meter thick PGM-rich horizon. Source of all US primary PGM production.

    Skaergaard Intrusion (East Greenland): Approximately 8 x 11 kilometers. Hosts PGM-gold mineralization in the Triple Group and other sulfide-rich horizons. One of the world's best-exposed and most-studied layered intrusions.

    Norilsk-Talnakh (Russia): Massive PGM-Ni-Cu deposits associated with the Siberian Traps flood basalt province. While technically not a classic layered intrusion, Norilsk's mineralization shares similar sulfide-collection characteristics.

    Why Skaergaard Is Geologically Significant

    Skaergaard is one of the most important layered intrusions in the world for several reasons:

  • **Exposure**: Skaergaard is unusually well-exposed (glacial erosion has stripped away covering rock), providing a complete cross-section through the intrusion. This makes it a type locality for understanding layered intrusion processes
  • **PGM enrichment**: The Triple Group at Skaergaard is one of the most PGM-enriched horizons known in any layered intrusion
  • **Gold content**: Unlike most PGM-bearing layered intrusions, Skaergaard has significant gold content, which is unusual and economically valuable
  • **Small but rich**: Skaergaard is much smaller than the Bushveld or Great Dyke, but its PGM grades are competitive, and the gold byproduct adds significant value

    • Exploration Implications

    Understanding layered intrusion geology helps evaluate exploration and mining risks:

  • **Layer continuity**: PGM-bearing layers in layered intrusions typically extend for kilometers along strike, providing geological continuity that supports resource confidence
  • **Grade predictability**: Once the geological framework is understood, grade distribution can be predicted with reasonable accuracy
  • **Stratigraphic control**: PGM mineralization is controlled by stratigraphy (rock layer position), making exploration targeting relatively straightforward compared to other deposit types
  • **Structural complexity**: Post-intrusion faulting can displace mineralized layers, creating exploration and mining complexity

    • Conclusion

    Layered mafic intrusions are the geological source of most of the world's PGMs. The process of sulfide saturation and PGM collection creates thin, laterally extensive horizons of enriched mineralization that can be mined economically. Skaergaard is one of the world's most significant layered intrusions, with PGM grades comparable to the Bushveld Complex and the added advantage of significant gold content. For investors, the layered intrusion geological model provides a well-understood framework for evaluating Skaergaard's resource and exploration potential.
