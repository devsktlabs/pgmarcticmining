PGM Recycling: The Secondary Supply That Cannot Fill the Gap

By Dr. Pavel Novak · 2026-03-23

pgm recyclingsecondary supplycatalytic converters

Recycling is a vital component of the PGM supply chain, providing approximately 25-30% of total palladium supply and a similar share of platinum supply. Without recycling, the PGM supply deficit would be even more severe. However, recycling alone cannot bridge the growing gap between primary supply and demand, making new mining supply essential.

Sources of Recycled PGMs

Automotive catalytic converters: The largest source of recycled PGMs. End-of-life vehicles are collected, catalytic converters are removed, and the PGMs are recovered through high-temperature processing:

  • Collection: End-of-life vehicles enter the recycling stream through scrapyards, dismantlers, and vehicle recycling facilities
  • Decanning: Catalytic converters are removed and the ceramic monolith (containing PGMs) is extracted from the steel casing
  • Processing: The monolith is crushed, milled, and processed through pyrometallurgical or hydrometallurgical methods to recover PGMs
  • Recovery: Modern recycling facilities achieve 90-95% recovery of PGMs from processed catalytic converters

Industrial recycling: PGMs are also recovered from industrial sources:

  • Chemical and petroleum catalysts: Spent catalysts from chemical plants and petroleum refineries
  • Electronic waste: PGMs recovered from printed circuit boards, hard drives, and other electronic components
  • Jewelry: PGM-bearing jewelry is melted down and refined

PGM-bearing scrap: Manufacturing scrap from PGM-using industries (catalyst production, jewelry manufacturing, electronics) is recycled with high efficiency.

Scale of PGM Recycling

The scale of PGM recycling has grown significantly over the past two decades:

  • Palladium recycling: Approximately 2.0-2.5 million ounces annually (approximately 25-30% of total palladium supply)
  • Platinum recycling: Approximately 1.8-2.2 million ounces annually (approximately 25-30% of total platinum supply)
  • Rhodium recycling: Approximately 0.2-0.3 million ounces annually (limited by the smaller total market and the technical difficulty of rhodium recovery)

Constraints on Recycling Growth

Despite its importance, recycling faces structural constraints that limit its ability to grow:

Vehicle fleet turnover: The primary source of recycled PGMs is end-of-life vehicles. The average vehicle lifespan is 12-15 years in developed markets and 15-20 years in emerging markets. This means that PGMs used in new vehicles today will not enter the recycling stream for 12-20 years. The recycling supply today reflects the PGM content of vehicles manufactured in the 2006-2014 period.

Collection efficiency: Not all end-of-life vehicles are properly recycled. In some markets, vehicles are abandoned, exported to countries without formal recycling infrastructure, or dismantled without catalytic converter recovery. Global collection rates are estimated at 70-80% for catalytic converters in developed markets, lower in emerging markets.

PGM loading decline: Automakers have progressively reduced PGM loading (the amount of PGM per catalytic converter) as engine efficiency and catalyst technology have improved. This means that future vehicles will contain less PGM per unit, reducing the future recycling yield per vehicle.

Technical complexity: Rhodium recovery from catalytic converters is technically more challenging than palladium or platinum recovery, limiting the growth of rhodium recycling.

The Recycling Forecast

PGM recycling is expected to grow gradually:

  • Short term (2026-2030): Recycling grows at 2-4% annually, driven by increasing vehicle scrappage rates in mature markets and improving collection infrastructure in emerging markets
  • Medium term (2030-2035): Growth accelerates as vehicles manufactured during the peak PGM-loading era (2005-2015) enter the recycling stream, but this effect is partially offset by declining PGM loading in newer vehicles
  • Long term (2035+): Recycling growth slows as the fleet mix shifts toward EVs with no catalytic converters, reducing the future pool of recyclable PGMs

Why New Mining Supply Is Still Needed

Even with optimistic recycling assumptions, new primary mining supply is needed:

  • Growing demand: Industrial demand for PGMs (chemical, petroleum, electronics, glass) continues to grow, and hydrogen fuel cell demand could add a significant new demand source
  • Recycling limitations: Recycling can never exceed the amount of PGMs originally placed in service. It is inherently a declining supply source once vehicle production of ICE vehicles declines
  • Supply concentration: Primary PGM supply is highly concentrated in South Africa and Russia. New mining supply from Western jurisdictions diversifies the supply base
  • Recycling quality: Recycled PGMs may not meet the purity specifications required for some high-technology applications

Skaergaard's Role Alongside Recycling

Skaergaard complements recycling rather than competing with it:

  • New supply: Skaergaard adds new primary supply that recycling cannot provide
  • Diversification: Skaergaard's Western jurisdiction provides supply diversification that recycled supply (concentrated in the regions where vehicles are scrapped) cannot match
  • Timing: Skaergaard's potential production timeline (early 2030s) coincides with a period when SA primary supply is likely to be declining further and recycling may be plateauing

Conclusion

PGM recycling provides critical secondary supply that reduces the need for primary mining. However, recycling faces structural constraints (vehicle fleet turnover, collection efficiency, declining PGM loading) that limit its ability to fill the growing supply-demand gap. New primary mining supply from projects like Skaergaard remains essential for meeting PGM demand in the medium and long term. For investors, Skaergaard's primary supply contribution is complementary to recycling, not redundant.
