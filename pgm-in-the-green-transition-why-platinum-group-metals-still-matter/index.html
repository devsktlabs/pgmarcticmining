Platinum Group Metals in the Green Transition: Why PGMs Still Matter

By Dr. Pavel Novak · 2026-03-23

pgmgreen transitionautocatalystclean energy

The narrative around platinum group metals and the energy transition is often reduced to a simple question: "What happens to PGM demand when EVs replace gasoline cars?" The reality is far more nuanced. PGMs play critical roles across multiple decarbonization pathways, and their importance may actually increase in certain applications even as automotive demand evolves.

PGMs in Automotive Emissions Control

The automotive sector remains the largest consumer of PGMs, using platinum, palladium, and rhodium in catalytic converters to reduce harmful emissions:

  • Palladium: Primarily used in gasoline engine catalytic converters
  • Platinum: Used in diesel engine catalytic converters and increasingly substituted for palladium in gasoline converters
  • Rhodium: Critical for NOx reduction in all types of catalytic converters

While EVs do not use catalytic converters, the automotive transition is more gradual than commonly assumed:

  • Global vehicle fleet: There are approximately 1.4 billion vehicles on the road globally. Even with rapid EV adoption, the majority of vehicles will continue to use internal combustion engines for at least 15-20 years
  • Hybrid vehicles: Plug-in hybrids (PHEVs) and conventional hybrids use catalytic converters and are growing in popularity as a transitional technology
  • Emerging markets: Vehicle ownership is growing rapidly in India, Southeast Asia, Africa, and parts of Latin America, where affordable ICE vehicles remain dominant
  • Heavy-duty vehicles: Trucks, buses, and construction equipment are slower to electrify and will continue to require catalytic converters

PGMs in Hydrogen Economy

PGMs are essential for hydrogen fuel cell technology, which many governments and industries view as a key component of the energy transition:

  • Platinum in fuel cells: Proton exchange membrane (PEM) fuel cells use platinum as a catalyst. Current fuel cell electric vehicles (FCEVs) use approximately 30-60 grams of platinum per vehicle
  • Electrolyzers: PEM electrolyzers (used to produce green hydrogen from renewable electricity) use platinum and iridium as catalysts
  • Hydrogen infrastructure: PGM-based sensors and catalysts are used in hydrogen production, storage, and distribution infrastructure

If the hydrogen economy develops as projected by organizations like the International Energy Agency, platinum demand from fuel cells and electrolyzers could reach 1-2 million ounces per year by 2035, creating a significant new demand source.

PGMs in Industrial Applications

PGMs have irreplaceable industrial applications:

  • Chemical industry: PGM catalysts are used in the production of nitric acid, benzene, and other chemicals
  • Petroleum refining: Platinum and palladium catalysts used in petroleum refining and reforming
  • Electronics: Palladium in multi-layer ceramic capacitors (MLCCs), the most common type of capacitor in electronic devices
  • Glass manufacturing: Platinum rhodium alloys used in glass fiber production for insulation and wind turbine blades
  • Medical: PGMs used in medical devices, dental alloys, and pharmaceutical catalysts

These industrial applications provide a base level of PGM demand that is relatively insensitive to the automotive transition.

The Supply Challenge

While demand for PGMs is evolving, supply faces structural constraints:

  • South Africa: Declining production from aging, deepening mines
  • Russia: Sanctions constrain Norilsk Nickel's production and market access
  • Recycling: Provides approximately 25-30% of total PGM supply, but growth is limited by vehicle fleet turnover
  • New supply: Few new PGM projects are in development globally. Skaergaard is one of the most significant new projects

The supply-demand gap is particularly acute for palladium and rhodium, where recycling and new supply cannot easily fill the gap left by declining primary production.

Skaergaard's PGMs in the Transition Context

Skaergaard's PGM production is relevant to the green transition in multiple ways:

  • Palladium for existing fleet: Palladium production supports catalytic converter manufacturing for the existing and near-term vehicle fleet
  • Platinum for fuel cells: If Skaergaard produces platinum alongside palladium (as a component of its PGM suite), it contributes to the emerging fuel cell supply chain
  • Rhodium for emissions control: Rhodium from Skaergaard supports NOx reduction in all types of catalytic converters
  • Gold as portfolio diversifier: The gold byproduct provides investment diversification that pure PGM companies cannot offer

Policy Support

Government policies increasingly recognize PGMs as critical minerals:

  • EU Critical Raw Materials Act: Lists palladium, platinum, and rhodium as critical raw materials
  • US critical mineral lists: Includes PGMs in critical mineral designations
  • IRA (US): Inflation Reduction Act incentives for domestic PGM production and supply chain development

These policy designations create funding, regulatory, and market incentives for PGM projects in Western jurisdictions.

Conclusion

The narrative that "EVs kill PGM demand" is too simplistic. While automotive demand for palladium will gradually decline as EVs gain market share, PGMs remain critical for emissions control in the existing fleet, hydrogen fuel cells, and numerous industrial applications. Skaergaard's PGM production is relevant to the green transition across multiple pathways, and the structural supply deficit in PGMs creates a favorable market environment for new Western supply. For investors, Skaergaard offers PGM exposure in a transition-aware context, where demand evolution creates both challenges and new opportunities.
