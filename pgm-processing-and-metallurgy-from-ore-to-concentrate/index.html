PGM Processing and Metallurgy: From Ore to Concentrate

By Dr. Pavel Novak · 2026-03-23

Platinum group metal processing is among the most complex in the mining industry. The minerals that host PGMs are extremely fine-grained and disseminated within the ore, requiring sophisticated metallurgical processes to achieve acceptable recovery rates. Understanding the processing pathway from ore to marketable metal is essential for evaluating project economics and technical risk.

PGM Mineralogy

PGMs occur in nature as a group of minerals including:

  • **Sulfides**: Cooperite (PtS), laurite (RuS2), braggite ((Pt,Pd,Ni)S)
  • **Alloys**: Isoferroplatinum (Pt3Fe), native platinum, native palladium
  • **Tellurides**: Moncheite ((Pt,Pd)(Te,Bi)2), kotulskite (PdTe)
  • **Arsenides and antimonides**: Sperrylite (PtAs2), genkinite ((Pt,Pd)4Sb)

    • These minerals are typically very fine-grained (micron-scale), occurring as inclusions within base metal sulfides (pyrrhotite, pentlandite, chalcopyrite) or as discrete grains in the silicate gangue. The fine grain size is the primary metallurgical challenge in PGM processing.

    The Processing Pathway

    Stage 1: Comminution (Crushing and Grinding)

    Ore must be ground to a fine size to liberate PGM minerals from the gangue:

  • **Crushing**: Primary, secondary, and tertiary crushing reduces ore from run-of-mine size to approximately 10-15mm
  • **Grinding**: SAG (semi-autogenous grinding) or AG (autogenous grinding) mills reduce ore to approximately 75-150 microns. For PGM deposits with very fine-grained mineralization, finer grinding may be required
  • **Energy intensity**: PGM grinding is energy-intensive due to the hard, abrasive nature of the ore. Grinding costs represent a significant portion of total processing costs

    • Stage 2: Flotation

    Flotation is the standard method for PGM recovery, using chemical reagents to create hydrophobic PGM particles that attach to air bubbles and float to the surface:

  • **Sulphide flotation**: PGMs are primarily associated with sulfide minerals, so flotation targets the sulfide fraction. Standard reagents include xanthates (collectors), frothers, and modifiers
  • **Rougher-scavenger-cleaner circuit**: A multi-stage flotation circuit progressively upgrades the concentrate grade while maximizing recovery
  • **Recovery targets**: 85-95% PGM recovery through flotation is typical for well-characterized deposits. Recovery varies by mineral type (sulfide-associated PGMs are easier to recover than silicate-associated PGMs)
  • **Concentrate grade**: PGM concentrates typically grade 100-300 grams per tonne combined PGMs, with grade and recovery trading off against each other

    • Stage 3: Concentrate Smelting

    The flotation concentrate is shipped to a smelter for further processing:

  • **Smelting**: High-temperature furnaces (electric arc furnaces or flash smelters) separate the PGM-bearing sulfide matte from the slag (waste silicate material)
  • **Converting**: The sulfide matte is converted to remove iron and sulfur, producing a PGM-rich matte
  • **Base metals removal**: Copper and nickel are removed through leaching and refining, producing a PGM-rich residue for further refining

    • Stage 4: Refining

    PGM refining separates individual metals from the PGM-rich residue:

  • **Solvent extraction and precipitation**: A series of chemical processes separate palladium, platinum, rhodium, iridium, ruthenium, and gold
  • **Final product**: Individual pure metals in the form of sponge, powder, or ingots
  • **Refineries**: Major PGM refineries include Johnson Matthey (UK), Heraeus (Germany), Umicore (Belgium), and Impala Refining (South Africa)

    • Skaergaard-Specific Processing

    Skaergaard's processing pathway benefits from its specific mineralogical characteristics:

  • **Sulfide-associated PGMs**: PGMs at Skaergaard are primarily associated with sulfide minerals, which are amenable to standard flotation processing
  • **Gold co-recovery**: Gold is recovered alongside PGMs through flotation, with potential additional gravity recovery for coarse gold
  • **Rhodium**: Rhodium is recovered in the PGM concentrate and separated at the refinery
  • **Metallurgical testing**: Comprehensive metallurgical test work has been conducted on Skaergaard ore, providing confidence in recovery assumptions

    • Processing Challenges

    Key metallurgical challenges for PGM processing include:

  • **Chrome interference**: Chromite (chromium oxide) can interfere with flotation by consuming reagents and depressing PGM recovery. Skaergaard's moderate chrome content should be manageable through standard circuit design
  • **Fine grain size**: Very fine-grained PGM minerals require fine grinding for liberation, increasing energy costs and potentially creating slimes that are difficult to float
  • **Grade variability**: If mined ore grade is more variable than predicted, processing circuit performance may vary
  • **Reagent costs**: PGM flotation reagents (collectors, activators, modifiers) are expensive, particularly for deposits requiring complex reagent schemes

    • Processing Costs

    PGM processing is relatively expensive compared to many other mineral processing operations:

  • **Grinding**: US$8-15 per tonne of ore
  • **Flotation**: US$5-10 per tonne of ore
  • **Total processing cost**: US$15-30 per tonne of ore processed

    • The high processing cost per tonne means that ore grade is critical: higher-grade ore produces more metal value per tonne of processing cost, improving economics.

    Conclusion

    PGM processing from ore to refined metal is a complex, multi-stage process that requires specialized metallurgical expertise and significant capital investment. Skaergaard's sulfide-associated PGM mineralization is amenable to standard flotation processing with expected recovery rates of 85-95%. The processing pathway is well-understood, with established smelters and refineries available to handle Skaergaard's concentrate. For investors, the metallurgical risk at Skaergaard is moderate, supported by comprehensive test work and a favorable mineralogical profile.
