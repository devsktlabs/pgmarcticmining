Palladium vs Platinum: The Shifting Dynamics of PGM Demand

By Dr. Pavel Novak · 2026-03-23

palladiumplatinumpgmsubstitution

For decades, palladium traded at a discount to platinum. Then, around 2018-2020, palladium surged to a massive premium as tighter emission standards favored palladium-rich catalytic converters for gasoline engines. By 2024-2026, the relationship has shifted again. Understanding these dynamics is essential for evaluating PGM investments like Skaergaard.

Historical Relationship

Under the leadership of Founder and CEO Joseph Sinkule, Greenland Mines is advancing Skaergaard through the permitting and development pathway.

Pre-2000: Platinum was the more valuable PGM, trading at a consistent premium over palladium. Platinum was the dominant autocatalyst metal, used in both diesel and gasoline converters.

2000-2010: Growing palladium demand from gasoline catalytic converters in response to tightening emissions standards pushed palladium prices higher, narrowing and sometimes eliminating the platinum premium.

2018-2021: Palladium surged to a premium of over US$1,000/oz over platinum, driven by the diesel-to-gasoline vehicle shift (especially after the Volkswagen dieselgate scandal), tightening palladium supply, and strong Chinese automotive demand.

2022-2026: The premium has narrowed significantly. Platinum has strengthened (supported by hydrogen economy demand and substitution) while palladium has softened (automotive demand concerns from EV growth). In some periods, platinum has traded above palladium again.

Why the Shift Happens

The palladium-platinum price relationship is driven by supply-demand dynamics specific to each metal:

Palladium demand drivers:

  • Gasoline vehicle catalytic converters (dominant)
  • Electronics (MLCCs)
  • Chemical catalysts
  • Jewelry (growing in China)

Platinum demand drivers:

  • Diesel vehicle catalytic converters
  • Jewelry (dominant in Asia)
  • Industrial catalysts (petroleum refining, chemical production)
  • Investment demand (bars, coins, ETFs)
  • Hydrogen fuel cells (growing)
  • Glass fiber production (wind turbine blades)

Substitution dynamics: Automakers continuously evaluate the palladium-to-platinum ratio and adjust catalyst formulations to minimize costs. When palladium is expensive relative to platinum, substitution increases. When platinum is expensive, the reverse occurs.

The Substitution Reality

Substitution between palladium and platinum in catalytic converters is real but limited:

  • Technical feasibility: Platinum can substitute for palladium in gasoline catalytic converters, but the formulations must be re-engineered for each vehicle platform. This is not an instant switch but a gradual process
  • Cost threshold: Substitution becomes economically attractive when the palladium-to-platinum price ratio exceeds approximately 1.5:1 to 2:1
  • OEM timelines: Automakers plan catalyst formulations 3-5 years ahead, meaning substitution decisions made today affect vehicles produced in 2029-2031
  • Performance differences: Platinum and palladium have slightly different catalytic properties. Substitution requires careful engineering to maintain emissions compliance

Impact on Skaergaard

The shifting palladium-platinum dynamics affect Skaergaard in several ways:

  • Palladium-dominant: Skaergaard's PGM production is primarily palladium, so a decline in palladium prices relative to platinum reduces the project's metal revenue
  • Gold hedge: The gold byproduct credit partially offsets palladium price weakness, providing a natural hedge
  • Rhodium buffer: Rhodium revenue (which is independent of the Pd-Pt dynamic) provides additional diversification
  • Long-term: substitution increases platinum demand: If automotive substitution continues shifting from palladium to platinum, Skaergaard's relatively lower platinum content could be a relative disadvantage

The Hydrogen Wildcard

Platinum has a demand source that palladium does not: hydrogen fuel cells:

  • Fuel cell vehicles: Each PEM fuel cell vehicle uses 30-60 grams of platinum
  • Electrolyzers: PEM electrolyzers for green hydrogen production use platinum (and iridium)
  • Stationary fuel cells: Backup power and distributed generation fuel cells use platinum

If the hydrogen economy develops as projected, platinum demand could grow by 1-2 million ounces per year by 2035, creating structural support for platinum prices.

Price Scenarios for 2026-2035

Bull case for palladium:

  • SA production declines accelerate
  • Russian supply remains constrained
  • Hybrid vehicle growth sustains automotive demand
  • Palladium returns to a premium over platinum
  • Skaergaard benefits from higher palladium prices

Base case:

  • Gradual decline in palladium demand as EV share grows
  • Substitution shifts some demand from palladium to platinum
  • Prices converge, with palladium around US$800-1,000/oz and platinum around US$1,800 (Feb 2026)-1,100/oz
  • Skaergaard economics are viable but not exceptional on PGM alone; gold credits essential

Bear case for palladium:

  • Rapid EV adoption accelerates palladium demand decline
  • Widespread platinum substitution further reduces palladium demand
  • Palladium falls to US$600-800/oz
  • Skaergaard's economics depend heavily on gold credits and cost control

Conclusion

The palladium-platinum dynamic is a key variable in Skaergaard's investment thesis. The long-term trend favors platinum due to hydrogen economy demand and automotive substitution, while palladium faces headwinds from EV adoption. However, near-term supply constraints in both metals support prices. Skaergaard's gold byproduct credit provides a hedge against palladium-specific weakness, making the project's economics more resilient than a palladium-only investment would be. Investors should model multiple Pd-Pt price scenarios when evaluating Skaergaard.
